Message from Founders

A letter from the people behind TKG

We did not start
with a product.
We started with a question.

May 2026 Thiện Kiến Founders

To anyone reading this —

We grew up with Vietnamese herbs the way most Vietnamese families do: not as a category, not as wellness, and certainly not as a product. They were just there — in the kitchen, in the morning drink someone's grandmother made, in the smell that came from a pot left simmering too long.

Ginger was for warmth. Lemongrass was for flavor and recovery. Perilla was for the end of the day when the body needed quiet. Nobody called them adaptogens or botanicals. They were just what people used, because they worked, and because their parents had used them before that.

When we began thinking about TKG, the question we kept returning to was a simple one: why, when Vietnamese herbs travel across borders, do they lose everything that made them worth using in the first place?

The answer, we found, is almost always the same. They get extracted, anonymised, reformulated, and flavoured back into something approximating their original character. The plant becomes an ingredient. The ingredient becomes a note. The note becomes a product.

We wanted to try something different — to start from the plant, name everything on the label, and build a preparation method around preserving what was actually there.

That is what TKG is. Not a brand built around a concept. A brand built around a practice — the Naula preparation discipline — applied consistently to Vietnamese botanicals that deserve to be recognized for what they actually are.

Thiện Kiến
Founders · TKG · Ho Chi Minh City, Vietnam
01 — The Gap We Saw

Recognized everywhere.
Understood nowhere.

Vietnamese herbs are known globally. Ginger, lemongrass, perilla — they appear on menus, in supplements, in supermarket aisles across Europe and Asia. But the version that travels is rarely the version that was actually used. What arrives is an extract, a concentrate, a powder made from something that may once have been a real plant but has since been processed into something uniform, stable, and forgettable.

The preparation traditions that made these herbs worth knowing — the specific heat, the specific timing, the respect for how each botanical behaves differently — rarely survive the journey. What remains is a suggestion of the original. A flavour rather than a substance.

"The plant becomes an ingredient. The ingredient becomes a note. The note becomes a product."

Naula preparation · Long An, Vietnam

02 — Why Naula

A method,
not a marketing name.

Naula is the name we gave to the preparation discipline at the center of TKG. It is not a brand concept invented for export. It is a structured approach to cooking and preserving Vietnamese botanical ingredients — one that adjusts the process for each herb individually rather than applying a single industrial method to all of them.

The core principle is straightforward: every botanical ingredient behaves differently under heat, water, and time. Ginger root requires different treatment than lemongrass. Perilla leaf reacts to pH in ways that clove does not. A method that works for one will compromise another. Naula is built around that recognition.

"We are not trying to control nature completely. We are trying to interfere with it less — and to preserve what was already there."

After preparation in liquid form, the extraction is preserved through freeze-drying — a process that removes moisture under deep cold and vacuum rather than heat. It is more expensive. It is slower. It preserves more of the aroma, color, and botanical character than alternatives. For us, there was no other serious option.

The result is a powder that dissolves in water and behaves like the herb it came from — including the parts that most processed products try to eliminate. The natural sedimentation. The color that shifts with pH. The taste that is not identical every single time because the ingredient it came from is a living thing.

03 — The Ingredient

Named on the label.
Every time.

One of the things that frustrated us about botanical products — including many we respected — was ingredient anonymity. "Natural flavoring." "Botanical extract." "Herbal blend." These phrases tell you nothing about where the ingredient came from, how it was processed, or whether the original plant was involved at all in any meaningful way.

At TKG, every ingredient is named. Not as a marketing strategy, but as a commitment. Ginger root. Lemongrass stalk. Lime peel. Perilla leaf. Clove. Kumquat. When you dissolve a serving of Morning Ember or Still Ember, you know exactly what you are drinking — because it says so on the label, and because nothing else has been added to confuse it.

We test through Quatest 3. We do not use artificial color, artificial sweetener, or preservatives. We keep the formulation as short as possible, because we believe a shorter ingredient list is usually a more honest one.

How we got here

A record of decisions made.

Early
The original question
Observing how Vietnamese herbs were exported and processed. Noticing the gap between how herbs were actually used in Vietnam and what arrived on the other side of the supply chain. Beginning to ask whether a different approach was viable.
Research
Building the Naula Method
Testing botanical preparation approaches. Identifying how each ingredient responds differently to heat, water ratio, and duration. Developing individual protocols for ginger, lemongrass, lime, perilla, clove, and kumquat. Choosing freeze-drying over spray-drying after understanding the trade-offs.
2025
First two products: Morning Ember & Still Ember
Launched the first two formulations under the Hệ Hỏa (Fire Element) line. Morning Ember: ginger, lemongrass, lime — a warm start. Still Ember: perilla, clove, kumquat — a quiet close. Laboratory tested through Quatest 3. No artificial additions. Products named, not branded.
2026
World of Naula — publishing the thinking
Beginning to document the practice in writing. Why freeze-drying. What the color change in Still Ember means. What Hệ Hỏa actually is. Writing slowly, publishing only when complete. The goal is not content — it is a record of decisions made and the reasoning behind them.
Next
Expanding the botanical range
Continuing to develop within the Vietnamese botanical vocabulary. More ingredients. More formulations. The same discipline. Nothing released before it is ready — and nothing released that contradicts the practice we have built.
